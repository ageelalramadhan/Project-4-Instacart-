Project Overview

Citi Bike generates millions of trip records every year, but raw trip logs don't tell operations teams when to scale supply, which stations to prioritize, or how to plan for seasonal swings. This project takes 1.7 million individual ride records, merges them with NOAA daily weather data from LaGuardia Airport, and turns the combined dataset into an interactive, deployed dashboard built for operational decision-making.

Business question: How should Citi Bike scale bike supply and rebalancing operations across the year — and which stations, seasons, and environmental factors drive that decision?

Streamlit Dashboard

The full analysis is delivered as a deployed multi-page Streamlit dashboard with sidebar navigation and cached data loading. The figures below show the live app's intro, station concentration, and weather pages.

Station Concentration: Where Demand Sits

Trip demand is heavily concentrated. The top 10 starting stations capture a disproportionate share of total trip starts — primarily clustering around major commuting hubs and Hudson River waterfront access points. W 21 St & 6 Ave leads at roughly 128,000 trips, with the rest of the top 10 sitting between 94K and 122K.

Key Insight — Rebalancing leverage is concentrated

Because trip starts cluster heavily at a small set of stations, rebalancing crews can drive disproportionate operational gains by prioritising redistribution to and from the top 10 stations rather than allocating attention uniformly.

Temperature Predicts Daily Ridership

Overlaying daily ride counts with NOAA average temperature shows the two signals track each other closely across the full year. Ridership climbs from a winter floor near 20K trips/day to summer peaks above 130K trips/day; cold snaps produce sharp drops, warm stretches push counts toward seasonal peaks.

Key Insight — Temperature as a planning signal

Daily ridership tracks closely with daily average temperature. Operations should use forecast temperature (not just calendar month) as a proximal signal for next-day demand scaling — particularly during shoulder seasons.

Key Findings

Finding 1 — Demand triples between winter and summer.

Daily rides scale ~3x from January (~33K) to August (~115K). Winter operations should run at roughly half of summer intensity, with a deliberate ramp from April onward.

Finding 2 — Temperature is a stronger planning signal than calendar month.

Forecast temperature predicts next-day ridership more precisely than month alone. Operational scaling should use temperature forecasts as a proximal demand input — especially during shoulder seasons.

Finding 3 — Demand is concentrated, so rebalancing leverage is concentrated.

The top 10 stations capture a disproportionate share of trip starts. Prioritizing redistribution at these stations yields higher operational ROI than uniform attention.

Finding 4 — A deployed dashboard converts ad-hoc analysis into recurring decisions.

Moving from one-off charts to a multi-page Streamlit app gives operations teams self-service access to seasonal, weather, and station insights — making the analysis directly actionable.
